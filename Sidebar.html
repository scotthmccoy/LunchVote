LUNCHVOTE

Given list of candidates and ranked ballots, calculates an election result.

Sample Usage

LUNCHVOTE(A2:A, C2:C, D2:D, E2:E)

Syntax

LUNCHVOTE(candidate_list, ballot_1, ballot_2, ... ballot_n)

  • candidate_list - a vertical range containing unique candidate names.

    • If known_data_y is a two-dimensional array or range,

  • ballot - a vertical range containing numerical representation of a voter's preference order for the candidates specified in candidate_list.

    • A lower number indicates greater preference. For example, If you ranked hypothetical candidates A, B, and C as 1, 2, 3, that means you prefer A to B, and B to C.
    • Any candidate not ranked on a ballot is considered tied for last place. For example, if you had candidates A through Z, and ranked A, B and C as 1, 2, and 3, that means you prefer A to B, B to C, and C to every other candidate.
    • You can use whatever numbers you like. If in the previous example you ranked A as 1337, B as 24601 and C as 999999, that still means you prefer A to B, B to C, and C to every other candidate.
    • Abstentions: If ballot is blank or does not contain any numerical values, it is considered an abstention and has no effect .

How Does It Work? and Further Reading

LunchVote uses the Shulze Method (pronounced SHULTZ, like the creator of Charlie Brown) to determine election outcomes. Shulze is a Condorcet Methods, a family of election methods that have the wonderful property of selecting the candidate that would beat every other candidate in a one-on-one election (if such a candidate exists). Though not everyone agrees that the Condorcet Methods are ideal (most notably, in my opinion, the folks at RangeVoting.org. I personally can't think of a case where if a Condorcet methods, Schulze was chosen over Ranked Pairs due to:

  1. Shulze allows for ties on ballots. For example, a voter could express that their #1 preference is Burgers, but their #2 preferences are either Tacos or Sushi. Ranked Pairs requires that all candidates be ranked with unique numbers which can be burdensome for voters when there are many candidates.
  2. Shulze runs in O(n^3) time while Ranked Pairs runs in O(n^4). This is a significant difference, especially for an algorithm implemented in JavaScript!
